Food Storage

Build your food storage gradually and be prepared for any situation

Getting Started

Building a food storage doesn't have to be overwhelming. Start small and build gradually. Focus on foods your family already eats and will actually use. Rotate your storage regularly to keep it fresh.

Three-Month Supply

Start Here

Build a supply of food that is part of your normal, daily diet. This supply should sustain life in case of temporary disruption.

  • Store foods you regularly eat
  • Include a variety of proteins, grains, fruits, and vegetables
  • Don't forget cooking essentials (oil, salt, spices)
  • Consider special dietary needs
  • Rotate regularly - use and replace

Long-Term Storage

For longer-term needs, focus on staples that can be stored for extended periods:

Grains

  • Wheat (hard red or white)
  • Rice (white stores longer)
  • Oats
  • Corn
  • Pasta

Legumes

  • Dry beans (various types)
  • Lentils
  • Split peas
  • Chickpeas

Other Essentials

  • Powdered milk
  • Sugar
  • Honey
  • Salt
  • Cooking oil

Canned Goods

  • Fruits and vegetables
  • Meats (chicken, tuna, beef)
  • Soups and stews
  • Peanut butter

Storage Conditions

Proper storage extends the life of your food:

  • Cool: Store at 75Â°F or below; cooler is better
  • Dry: Keep humidity low to prevent mold and spoilage
  • Dark: Light degrades food quality over time
  • Airtight: Use proper containers to prevent pests and oxidation
  • Off the floor: Store on shelves or pallets

Storage Containers

Short-Term

  • Original packaging
  • Food-grade plastic containers
  • Glass jars

Long-Term

  • #10 cans
  • Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers
  • Food-grade buckets
  • Vacuum-sealed bags

Shelf Life Guide

Food Item Shelf Life (Properly Stored)
Wheat 30+ years
White Rice 30+ years
Rolled Oats 30+ years
Dry Beans 30+ years
Sugar Indefinite
Honey Indefinite
Salt Indefinite
Powdered Milk 20+ years
Canned Goods 2-5 years
Pasta 8-10 years

Tips for Success

  • Start small: Add a few extra items each shopping trip
  • Store what you eat: Don't buy food your family won't use
  • Rotate regularly: Use the oldest items first
  • Keep an inventory: Know what you have and when it expires
  • Learn to prepare: Practice cooking with storage foods
  • Consider nutrition: Include vitamins and supplements
